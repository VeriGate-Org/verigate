Section 01

What Is SITA

Mandate and Legal Basis

The State Information Technology Agency (SITA) is a South African state-owned enterprise established in 1999 under the SITA Act (No. 88 of 1998). It falls under the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT).

SITA's mandate is to:

  • Consolidate and coordinate the State's information technology resources to achieve cost savings through economies of scale
  • Increase delivery capabilities of IT services to government
  • Enhance interoperability across government systems
  • Manage the IT procurement and delivery process to ensure government gets value for money
  • Leverage ICT as a strategic resource for government, improving public service delivery

Role in Government IT Procurement

SITA is the mandatory procurement agency for all national and provincial government IT requirements. Under the SITA Act and its regulations:

  • All government departments must approach SITA first for ICT goods and services procurement
  • The Auditor-General treats procurement outside SITA as irregular expenditure (though recent amendments have introduced exceptions — see Section 9)
  • SITA negotiates transversal contracts (bulk agreements) on behalf of all government departments
  • SITA manages the entire supply chain: from needs assessment, solicitation, supplier selection, bid adjudication, through to contract administration

Why Registration Matters

Key Insight

If Arthmatic DevWorks wants to sell VeriGate (its real-time risk intelligence platform) or any IT services to national or provincial government departments, SITA is the gatekeeper. Government departments are legally obligated to procure IT through SITA (with limited recent exceptions).

Being registered and visible in the correct procurement channels is essential for:

  • Being discoverable when departments raise IT requirements through SITA
  • Qualifying to respond to SITA RFQs (Requests for Quotation) and RFBs (Requests for Bid)
  • Getting listed on transversal contracts or panels, which guarantee ongoing procurement flow
  • Meeting compliance requirements that are verified before any contract is awarded
Section 02

How to Register as a SITA Supplier

Critical Finding

SITA does not maintain its own supplier register. There is no "SITA supplier registration form" to fill in. Instead, SITA uses the Central Supplier Database (CSD) managed by National Treasury as its supplier information source.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Register on the Central Supplier Database (CSD)

    This is the foundational requirement. All potential SITA suppliers must self-register at secure.csd.gov.za.

    1. Create a user account — Register with your email address and cell phone number
    2. Create your organisation profile — Enter company/business information including:
      • Company registration number (from CIPC)
      • Tax reference number (from SARS)
      • VAT number (if registered)
      • Company directors and shareholders
      • B-BBEE status
      • Banking details
      • Contact information
      • Business services/commodities you offer
    3. Submit for verification — The CSD system automatically verifies against SARS, CIPC, Department of Home Affairs, and your bank
    4. Receive your CSD number and unique reference code — These are required when responding to any SITA tender
  2. Register on SITA's e-Procurement Platform (g-Commerce)

    SITA operates the g-Commerce platform (gcommerce.gov.za) — a fully integrated source-to-pay solution for government procurement.

    1. Go to the g-Commerce portal
    2. Register with your username and password
    3. Complete the supplier profile, linking to your CSD credentials
  3. Monitor and Respond to Tenders

    SITA does not have a pre-qualification or pre-approval step. Instead:

    1. Continuously monitor tender opportunities at:
      • sita.co.za/content/invitations (SITA's own tender page)
      • etenders.gov.za (National Treasury's eTender portal)
      • Government Gazette tender bulletins
    2. Respond to relevant RFQs, RFBs, or RFPs when published
    3. Include your CSD number and unique reference code in every bid submission
  4. Technology Certification Process (TCP) — Optional

    If VeriGate includes hardware components or you are an OEM, SITA has a Technology Certification Process (TCP). This is primarily for hardware OEMs (PCs, servers, networking equipment), not for software/SaaS products.

    VeriGate Note

    For VeriGate (a software/SaaS verification platform), the TCP is likely not applicable. TCP contact: tas@sita.co.za

Additional Requirements for Bidding on SITA Tenders

When actually responding to SITA tenders (beyond CSD registration), you will typically need:

  • CSD number and unique reference code (mandatory on every bid)
  • Completed SBD (Standard Bidding Document) forms — declarations of interest, past supply chain practices, etc.
  • Technical proposal addressing the specific tender specifications
  • Pricing schedule as per the tender format
  • Proof of relevant experience and reference letters
  • MIOS compliance — Minimum Interoperability Standards for government IT
  • MISS compliance — Minimum Information Security Standards
  • Professional indemnity insurance (often required for IT services contracts)

B-BBEE Considerations

B-BBEE is not legally mandatory to register on CSD, but it is practically essential for competitiveness. Government procurement uses a Preferential Procurement Framework where B-BBEE status directly affects scoring:

  • Contract award and negotiation: additional 30–60 days

Getting on a Transversal Contract

Transversal contracts are awarded through competitive bidding and typically have multi-year terms (3–5 years). You can only join when SITA issues a new RFB or refreshes an existing contract. The supplier list is refreshed annually by SITA.

Section 07

SITA Transversal Contracts

What Are Transversal Contracts?

Transversal contracts are centralised, multi-department agreements that National Treasury and SITA facilitate for goods or services that multiple government institutions require. They are:

  • Negotiated centrally to leverage government's collective purchasing power
  • Mandatory for government departments to use (where applicable)
  • Available to all national departments, provincial departments, parastatals, and organs of state
  • Designed to standardise pricing, terms, and quality across government

Legal Basis

  • Public Finance Management Act (PFMA)
  • SITA Act (No. 88 of 1998)
  • Section 217 of the Constitution — requiring fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective procurement

Do You Need CSD First?

Answer

Yes, absolutely. CSD registration is a prerequisite for any engagement with SITA. You cannot respond to any SITA RFQ or RFB without a CSD number. Your CSD number and unique reference code must appear on every bid submission.

How They Interact

  1. You register on CSD (self-service, once)
  2. SITA publishes a tender on its website and the eTender portal
  3. You respond to the tender, including your CSD number
  4. SITA evaluates your bid and checks your CSD information (tax compliance, B-BBEE status, company validity) in real-time
  5. CSD verification happens automatically — no need to resubmit tax clearance certificates or B-BBEE documents each time

Key Benefit

Once registered on CSD, you are visible to all government entities, not just SITA. This opens doors to:

  • Direct departmental procurement
  • Municipal procurement
  • SOE procurement (Eskom, Transnet, etc.)
  • Provincial government procurement
Section 09

Recent Changes (2024–2026)

Key Opportunity

Major Reform: Departments Can Now Procure IT Independently

The most significant change affecting IT suppliers in 2025–2026 is the amendment to SITA regulations by Minister Solly Malatsi (DCDT):

  • Previously: Government departments were strictly required to procure all IT through SITA. Procurement outside SITA was flagged as irregular expenditure.
  • Now (from mid-2025): Departments have been empowered to procure digital services independently where there is a compelling business case for better value and faster turnaround.

How the New Rules Work

  1. A department identifies an IT need
  2. The department must still approach SITA first
  3. The department provides SITA with a business case, user requirement specifications, and a required delivery timeline
  4. SITA has 10 working days to respond, indicating whether it can fulfill the requirement within the specified timeframe and budget
  5. If SITA cannot meet the requirement, the department may procure independently through standard PFMA procurement processes
  6. The department must demonstrate: faster turnaround time, lower cost, and compliance with procurement prescripts

Why This Happened

This reform was driven by:

  • Mounting governance concerns at SITA
  • Irregular procurement practices within SITA itself
  • Operational inefficiencies and alarming deterioration in service delivery
  • SITA being a bottleneck that delayed critical government IT projects
What This Means for Arthmatic DevWorks

This is very good news. It means:

  • Departments can now approach VeriGate directly (after giving SITA first right of refusal)
  • The previous SITA monopoly bottleneck is loosening
  • Departments frustrated with SITA's slow turnaround times now have a legal pathway to work with smaller, agile IT companies
  • Building relationships directly with department CIOs and IT managers becomes a viable strategy
  • VeriGate's conflict of interest detection is particularly compelling for departments under Auditor-General scrutiny for procurement irregularities and undisclosed relationships — it directly addresses a governance gap that no existing SITA-contracted solution fills

Other Recent Developments

Automated Procurement Platform

SITA has soft-launched an automated supply chain management platform to streamline procurement workflows, incorporate predictive analytics, reduce administrative overhead, provide real-time reporting and audit trails, and improve supplier engagement.

g-Commerce Platform

SITA and National Treasury developed the g-Commerce platform — a fully integrated source-to-pay solution with an e-catalogue portal for transversal contracts, improved transaction tracking, and better price visibility.

Digitech SMME Platform

The DCDT and SITA established the Digitech SMME platform, which provides an inclusive marketplace for SMMEs to showcase products and services, connects them with government and private sector customers, and aims to expand reach locally and globally.

SITA Innovation Centre

Located at SITA's Centurion offices, the Innovation Centre provides facilities for South African innovators and SMMEs in the ICT industry, creating avenues for collaboration and development.

GovTech 2025 Conference

At GovTech 2025, SITA unveiled plans for a Citizen Super App for government services, strategic partnerships with Microsoft and DCDT for paperless government, and continued digital transformation of public services.

Alternative and Complementary Routes

Route 1 — NEW

Direct Departmental Engagement

With the amended SITA regulations, departments can now procure IT independently after giving SITA first right of refusal. Identify target departments with verification needs (Home Affairs, SAPS, Justice, Social Development), build relationships with CIOs and IT directors, and position VeriGate as a solution.

Route 2

eTender Portal Monitoring

Register and actively monitor the eTender Publication Portal at etenders.gov.za. Set up alerts for keywords: "verification," "identity," "gateway," "API," "software," "SaaS," "security."

Route 3

Provincial Government Procurement

Provincial departments (especially Western Cape, where Arthmatic is based) often have their own IT procurement processes. The WCG has its own e-procurement system with delegated authority thresholds.

Route 4

Municipal Procurement

Municipalities are not bound by the SITA Act. They can procure IT directly. Metro municipalities (Cape Town, Johannesburg, etc.) have substantial IT budgets. Register on municipal supplier databases directly.

Route 5

SOE and Public Entity Procurement

State-owned enterprises (SARS, CIPC, NPA, etc.) may have their own procurement processes and maintain separate supplier registration portals. SARS could be a strong target for VeriGate.

Route 6

Subcontracting to Panel Holders

Partner with or subcontract through a company already on the RFB 1183 panel. Many larger IT companies need specialised technology partners. Gets VeriGate into government projects faster.

Route 7

Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs)

For larger, longer-term engagements through National Treasury's PPP Unit. Suitable for ongoing verification services delivered as a managed service. Contact: +27 12 315 5741.

Route 8

SITA Innovation Centre / Digitech SMME

Engage with SITA's SMME development initiatives. Apply to showcase VeriGate at the SITA Innovation Centre (Centurion), register on the Digitech SMME platform, and participate in GovTech events.

Section 12

Tips and Common Pitfalls

Tips for Arthmatic DevWorks

1. Register on CSD immediately

This is free, takes 5–10 business days, and is the non-negotiable first step. Do not wait until you see a relevant tender.

2. Keep CSD information current

Procurement officials verify your information in real-time during bid evaluation. Outdated tax compliance or incorrect banking details will disqualify you automatically.

3. Get your B-BBEE right

As an SMME, you can use a sworn affidavit (free) if your turnover is below R10 million (EME) or a verification certificate if between R10–50 million (QSE). A strong B-BBEE score (Level 1–4) is effectively a prerequisite.

4. Select UNSPSC codes carefully on CSD

Choose codes that match VeriGate's capabilities: identity verification, access control, software services, security consulting. These codes determine which tender notifications you receive.

5. Monitor tenders actively

Set up daily monitoring of sita.co.za/content/invitations and etenders.gov.za. Relevant tenders can have 21–45 day response windows — you need to spot them early.

6. Attend compulsory briefing sessions

Many SITA tenders have compulsory briefing sessions. Missing one disqualifies your bid.

7. Build department relationships before the tender

Government procurement is relationship-driven. Identify departments that need verification services (Home Affairs, SAPS, DHA, Social Development, SARS) and engage their IT leadership. When a tender is published, you want to already be a known entity.

8. Position VeriGate within SITA's language

Frame your product using SITA's terminology: "Specialised Identity Management Services," "Specialised Access Control Services," "Business Solution Development." Match the RFB 1183 service definitions.

9. Consider subcontracting initially

Partner with a company already on the RFB 1183 transversal contract. This gets VeriGate into government projects faster while you build your own track record.

10. Leverage the 2025 SITA reform

The new regulations allowing departments to procure independently are a significant opportunity for agile SMMEs. Target departments frustrated with SITA's turnaround times.

11. Comply with MIOS and MISS

Ensure VeriGate meets the Minimum Interoperability Standards and Minimum Information Security Standards. Non-compliance is a disqualifier.

12. Prepare a standard government response template

Government bids require extensive documentation (SBD forms, declarations, technical proposals). Having templates ready saves time when a relevant tender drops.

Common Pitfalls

1. Assuming SITA has a separate supplier register

They do not. CSD is the register. Do not waste time looking for a SITA-specific registration form.

2. Expired tax compliance

SARS TCS status is verified in real-time. If your tax affairs are not in order, your bid is automatically disqualified regardless of technical merit.

3. Incorrect or outdated CSD information

Non-existing or outdated supplier records are a known problem on CSD. Keep your profile active and accurate.

4. Missing the transversal contract window

RFB 1183 refreshes happen periodically (not on a fixed schedule). If you miss the application window, you may wait 1–2 years for the next one. Monitor SITA's procurement page constantly.

5. Underestimating B-BBEE impact

The 20-point (or 10-point) B-BBEE scoring component can make or break a bid. A Level 1 B-BBEE contributor gets maximum preference points.

6. Not attending compulsory briefing sessions

This is an automatic disqualification. There is no exception.

7. Submitting late

Government procurement has strict deadlines. Late submissions are not accepted under any circumstances.

8. Not understanding the threshold rules

Different procurement rules apply based on value: RFQs for lower values, full tender processes for higher values. The R500,000 threshold on transversal contracts triggers different competitive requirements.

9. Ignoring provincial presence requirements

SITA transversal contracts accredit suppliers per province. If you are registered only in Western Cape, you may not be eligible for Gauteng-based opportunities. Consider multi-province accreditation.

10. Treating government procurement like private sector sales

Government procurement is process-driven, compliance-heavy, and slow. Patience, documentation rigour, and procedural adherence are essential.
